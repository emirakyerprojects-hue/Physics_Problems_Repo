# Task 07 – Motion in a central field

## Problem Statement

For the electric field:

$$
\vec{E}(r) = k \frac{Q}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$

1. Write the equation of motion of the particle.
2. Consider the case of radial motion.
3. Implement the RK4 method (mathematical formulation).
4. Investigate the case of positive and negative energy.
5. Compare with the gravitational analogy.

Requirement: animation of the motion trajectory.

## Theory

A particle of mass $m$ and charge $q$ in a central electric field generated by a stationary charge $Q$ experiences a Coulomb force:

$$
\vec{F} = q\vec{E} = k \frac{qQ}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$

By Newton's second law, the equation of motion is:

$$
m \frac{d^2\vec{r}}{dt^2} = k \frac{qQ}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$

Because this is a conservative central force, the total mechanical energy $E$ and angular momentum $L$ of the system are conserved. The total energy is the sum of kinetic and potential energy:

$$
E = E_k + U = \frac{1}{2} m |\vec{v}|^2 + k \frac{qQ}{r}
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Equation of motion

To solve the trajectory in a 2D plane, we express the position vector as $\vec{r} = (x, y)$ and the distance as $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$. The unit vector is $\hat{r} = \frac{\vec{r}}{r}$.

The vector equation of motion expands to:

$$
m \frac{d^2\vec{r}}{dt^2} = k \frac{qQ}{r^3} \vec{r}
$$

Separating into Cartesian components yields a system of second-order differential equations:

$$
\frac{d^2 x}{dt^2} = \frac{kqQ}{m} \frac{x}{(x^2 + y^2)^{3/2}}
$$

$$
\frac{d^2 y}{dt^2} = \frac{kqQ}{m} \frac{y}{(x^2 + y^2)^{3/2}}
$$

### 2. Radial motion

If the particle is released from rest or given an initial velocity purely in the radial direction, the angular momentum is zero ($L = 0$). The motion is strictly one-dimensional along the radial axis. 

The equation of motion simplifies to a single scalar equation:

$$
\frac{d^2 r}{dt^2} = \frac{kqQ}{m} \frac{1}{r^2}
$$

### 3. Implementation of the RK4 method

To apply the Runge-Kutta 4th order (RK4) method, the system of two second-order differential equations must be reduced to four first-order differential equations. 

We define the state vector $\vec{S}$:

$$
\vec{S} =
\begin{pmatrix}
x \\
y \\
v_x \\
v_y
\end{pmatrix}
$$

The derivative of the state vector with respect to time is $\vec{f}(t, \vec{S})$:

$$
\frac{d\vec{S}}{dt} = \vec{f}(t, \vec{S}) =
\begin{pmatrix}
v_x \\
v_y \\
\frac{kqQ}{m} \frac{x}{(x^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} \\
\frac{kqQ}{m} \frac{y}{(x^2 + y^2)^{3/2}}
\end{pmatrix}
$$

The RK4 update rule to advance the state from $t_n$ to $t_{n+1} = t_n + \Delta t$ is:

$$
\vec{S}_{n+1} = \vec{S}_n + \frac{\Delta t}{6} (\vec{k}_1 + 2\vec{k}_2 + 2\vec{k}_3 + \vec{k}_4)
$$

where the intermediate slopes are evaluated as:

$$
\begin{align}
\vec{k}_1 &= \vec{f}(t_n, \vec{S}_n) \\
\vec{k}_2 &= \vec{f}\left(t_n + \frac{\Delta t}{2}, \vec{S}_n + \frac{\Delta t}{2} \vec{k}_1\right) \\
\vec{k}_3 &= \vec{f}\left(t_n + \frac{\Delta t}{2}, \vec{S}_n + \frac{\Delta t}{2} \vec{k}_2\right) \\
\vec{k}_4 &= \vec{f}(t_n + \Delta t, \vec{S}_n + \Delta t \vec{k}_3)
\end{align}
$$

### 4. Positive and negative energy

The total energy dictates the nature of the trajectory:

$$
E = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 + k \frac{qQ}{r}
$$

* **Negative Energy ($E < 0$):** This can only occur if the charges have opposite signs ($qQ < 0$), making the potential energy negative. If $|U| > E_k$, the total energy is negative. The particle is **bound** to the central charge and the trajectory is an ellipse (or a circle as a special case).
* **Zero Energy ($E = 0$):** The kinetic energy exactly balances the potential well depth. The particle escapes to infinity on a parabolic trajectory.
* **Positive Energy ($E > 0$):** This occurs if the charges have the same sign (repulsive force, $U > 0$) or if the kinetic energy of an oppositely charged particle is very high. The particle is **unbound** and escapes to infinity on a hyperbolic trajectory.

### 5. Gravitational analogy

Newton's law of universal gravitation has the exact same mathematical form as Coulomb's law for a central field. 

The electrostatic force is:

$$
\vec{F}_e = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$

The gravitational force is:

$$
\vec{F}_g = -G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$

By substituting $k q_1 q_2 \to -G m_1 m_2$, the solutions to the electrostatic equations of motion map perfectly onto the solutions of orbital mechanics (Kepler's laws). 

## Final Result

1. Vector equation: $m \ddot{\vec{r}} = \frac{kqQ}{r^3} \vec{r}$.
2. Radial equation: $\ddot{r} = \frac{kqQ}{mr^2}$.
3. RK4 implementation requires evaluating the 4-component state vector derivative.
4. $E < 0$ results in bound elliptical orbits; $E \ge 0$ results in unbound hyperbolic/parabolic scattering.
5. The system perfectly mirrors Newtonian gravity, with the caveat that electrostatic forces can be repulsive.

## Interpretation

The $1/r^2$ central field is one of the most fundamental systems in physics. Because the force strictly points along the radial vector, torque is zero, leading to the conservation of angular momentum and confining the motion to a 2D plane. The total energy dictates whether the system forms a bound state (like an electron orbiting a nucleus or a planet orbiting a star) or a scattering state (like an alpha particle deflected by a heavy nucleus). The Runge-Kutta numerical method allows us to approximate these exact analytical solutions (conic sections) on a computer by iteratively summing small, corrected tangential steps.
