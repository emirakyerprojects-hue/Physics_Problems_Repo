# Task 06 – Cycloid: trajectory of a point on a rolling circle

## Problem Statement

A circle of radius $R$ rolls without slipping along the $x$-axis. The trajectory of a point on its rim traces a cycloid: 

$$
x(t) = R(\omega t - \sin(\omega t)), \qquad y(t) = R(1 - \cos(\omega t))
$$

Determine:
1. The velocity vector $\vec v(t)$ and the acceleration $\vec a(t)$.
2. The magnitude $|\vec v(t)|$ and the moments when the point temporarily stops relative to the ground.
3. The maximum values of $|\vec v|$ and $|\vec a|$.
4. The setup for an HTML animation.
5. A physical comparison with circular motion in the moving frame.

## Theory

A cycloid represents the path of a specific point strictly attached to the circumference of a uniformly rolling circle. Rolling without slipping dictates that the linear velocity of the circle's center is strictly coupled to its angular velocity via $v_{cm} = R\omega$.

Kinematically, the motion is a superposition of the uniform translation of the center of mass and the uniform rotation of the point around the center of mass.

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Velocity and Acceleration

Differentiate the position coordinates to find velocity:

$$
\begin{align}
\vec v(t) &= \left( \frac{d}{dt}\bigl[R(\omega t - \sin(\omega t))\bigr], \frac{d}{dt}\bigl[R(1 - \cos(\omega t))\bigr] \right) \\
          &= \bigl( R\omega - R\omega\cos(\omega t), R\omega\sin(\omega t) \bigr) \\
          &= \bigl( R\omega(1 - \cos(\omega t)), R\omega\sin(\omega t) \bigr)
\end{align}
$$

Differentiate the velocity to find acceleration:

$$
\begin{align}
\vec a(t) &= \left( \frac{d}{dt}\bigl[R\omega(1 - \cos(\omega t))\bigr], \frac{d}{dt}\bigl[R\omega\sin(\omega t)\bigr] \right) \\
          &= \bigl( R\omega^2\sin(\omega t), R\omega^2\cos(\omega t) \bigr)
\end{align}
$$

### 2. Velocity Magnitude and Stopping Points

Calculate the magnitude of the velocity vector:

$$
\begin{align}
|\vec v(t)| &= \sqrt{\bigl[R\omega(1 - \cos(\omega t))\bigr]^2 + \bigl[R\omega\sin(\omega t)\bigr]^2} \\
            &= \sqrt{R^2\omega^2 (1 - 2\cos(\omega t) + \cos^2(\omega t)) + R^2\omega^2\sin^2(\omega t)} \\
            &= R\omega \sqrt{1 - 2\cos(\omega t) + \cos^2(\omega t) + \sin^2(\omega t)} \\
            &= R\omega \sqrt{2 - 2\cos(\omega t)}
\end{align}
$$

Using the half-angle trigonometric identity $1 - \cos(\omega t) = 2\sin^2\left(\frac{\omega t}{2}\right)$:

$$
\begin{align}
|\vec v(t)| &= R\omega \sqrt{4\sin^2\left(\frac{\omega t}{2}\right)} \\
            &= 2R\omega \left| \sin\left(\frac{\omega t}{2}\right) \right|
\end{align}
$$

The point temporarily stops relative to the ground when $|\vec v(t)| = 0$:

$$
1 - \cos(\omega t) = 0 \implies \cos(\omega t) = 1
$$

$$
\omega t = 2k\pi \quad \text{for integers } k = 0, 1, 2, \dots
$$

At these instances, the point is physically touching the $x$-axis ($y = 0$).

### 3. Maximum Kinematic Magnitudes

**Maximum Velocity:**
The speed is bounded by the sine function, which has a maximum value of $1$.

$$
|\vec v|_{max} = 2R\omega
$$

This occurs when $\omega t = \pi, 3\pi, \dots$ (when the point is at the highest position, $y = 2R$).

**Maximum Acceleration:**
Calculate the magnitude of the acceleration vector:

$$
\begin{align}
|\vec a(t)| &= \sqrt{\bigl(R\omega^2\sin(\omega t)\bigr)^2 + \bigl(R\omega^2\cos(\omega t)\bigr)^2} \\
            &= R\omega^2 \sqrt{\sin^2(\omega t) + \cos^2(\omega t)} \\
            &= R\omega^2
\end{align}
$$

The acceleration magnitude is constant throughout the motion.

$$
|\vec a|_{max} = R\omega^2
$$

### 4. Animation Setup

To visualize this dynamically in HTML/JS:
- A center coordinate translating uniformly: $(x_c, y_c) = (R\omega t, R)$.
- A rotating frame superimposed: $(x', y') = (-R\sin(\omega t), -R\cos(\omega t))$.
- The absolute position is the vector sum of both frames. The locus of points left by the absolute position traces the characteristic scalloped arches of a cycloid.

### 5. Moving Reference Frame Comparison

In a non-rotating reference frame attached to the center of the rolling circle:
- The coordinate center translates with velocity $\vec v_c = (R\omega, 0)$.
- The point's relative coordinates describe pure circular motion: $\vec r_{rel} = (-R\sin(\omega t), -R\cos(\omega t))$.
- The point's relative acceleration is strictly centripetal with magnitude $R\omega^2$. Since the translating frame has zero acceleration, the absolute acceleration observed from the ground is strictly equal to the centripetal acceleration generated by the rotation, explaining why $|\vec a(t)| = R\omega^2$ is constant everywhere along the cycloid.

## Final Result

- $\vec v(t) = \bigl( R\omega(1 - \cos(\omega t)), R\omega\sin(\omega t) \bigr)$
- $\vec a(t) = \bigl( R\omega^2\sin(\omega t), R\omega^2\cos(\omega t) \bigr)$
- $|\vec v(t)| = 2R\omega \left| \sin\left(\frac{\omega t}{2}\right) \right|$
- Stops at $\omega t = 2k\pi$ (when touching the ground).
- $|\vec v|_{max} = 2R\omega$
- $|\vec a(t)| = R\omega^2$ (Constant)

## Interpretation

The cycloid mathematically demonstrates the principle of no-slip rolling. At the contact point with the ground, the backward tangential velocity from rotation exactly cancels the forward translational velocity of the center, resulting in an instantaneous velocity of zero. At the apex, the velocities constructively interfere, doubling the translational speed.
