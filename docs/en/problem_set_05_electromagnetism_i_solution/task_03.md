# Task 03 – Field at a point from a system of charges

## Problem Statement

Two point charges are given:
* $+q$ at point $(-a, 0)$
* $+2q$ at point $(a, 0)$

Answer the following questions:
1. Determine the field vector $\vec E(0, y)$, $\vec E(x, 0)$ and generally $\vec E(x, y)$.
2. Determine the condition for which the components $E_x = 0$, $E_y = 0$ and the zero field $\vec E = 0$.
3. Calculate the field for: $a = 0.2 \ \mathrm{m}$, $y = 0.3 \ \mathrm{m}$, $q = 2 \ \mu\mathrm{C}$.
4. Investigate the limit $y \gg a$.
5. Does a point of zero field exist on the $y$-axis?

## Theory

By the superposition principle, the total electric field $\vec{E}$ at a point $\vec{r} = (x, y)$ is the vector sum of the fields generated by each point charge.

$$
\vec{E}(\vec{r}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} k \frac{q_i}{|\vec{r} - \vec{r}_i|^3} (\vec{r} - \vec{r}_i)
$$

For this system, the position vectors of the charges are:

$$
\vec{r}_1 = (-a, 0), \quad \vec{r}_2 = (a, 0)
$$

The vectors pointing from the charges to the observation point $(x, y)$ are:

$$
\vec{r} - \vec{r}_1 = (x + a, y)
$$

$$
\vec{r} - \vec{r}_2 = (x - a, y)
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. General and specific field expressions

Substituting the specific coordinates into the superposition formula gives the general field $\vec{E}(x, y)$:

$$
\begin{align}
E_x(x, y) &= k q \left[ \frac{x + a}{\left((x + a)^2 + y^2\right)^{3/2}} + \frac{2(x - a)}{\left((x - a)^2 + y^2\right)^{3/2}} \right] \\
E_y(x, y) &= k q y \left[ \frac{1}{\left((x + a)^2 + y^2\right)^{3/2}} + \frac{2}{\left((x - a)^2 + y^2\right)^{3/2}} \right]
\end{align}
$$

To find the field on the x-axis, substitute $y = 0$:

$$
\begin{align}
E_x(x, 0) &= k q \left[ \frac{x + a}{|x + a|^3} + \frac{2(x - a)}{|x - a|^3} \right] \\
E_y(x, 0) &= 0
\end{align}
$$

To find the field on the y-axis, substitute $x = 0$:

$$
\begin{align}
E_x(0, y) &= k q \left[ \frac{a}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} - \frac{2a}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} \right] = -\frac{k q a}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} \\
E_y(0, y) &= k q y \left[ \frac{1}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} + \frac{2}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} \right] = \frac{3 k q y}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}}
\end{align}
$$

### 2. Conditions for zero components and zero field

The condition for $E_y = 0$ is immediately satisfied when $y = 0$. The terms inside the bracket for $E_y(x,y)$ are strictly positive, thus $y$ must be zero.

For the total field to be zero ($\vec{E} = 0$), both $E_x = 0$ and $E_y = 0$ must hold. Since $E_y = 0$ forces $y = 0$, the point must lie on the x-axis. Because both charges are positive, the zero field point must lie between them, where their fields point in opposite directions. This restricts the domain to $x \in (-a, a)$.

In this region, $x + a > 0$ and $x - a < 0$. Setting $E_x(x, 0) = 0$:

$$
k q \left( \frac{1}{(x + a)^2} - \frac{2}{(x - a)^2} \right) = 0
$$

$$
\frac{1}{(x + a)^2} = \frac{2}{(x - a)^2}
$$

Taking the square root of both sides (noting the signs of the terms in the domain):

$$
\frac{1}{x + a} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{a - x}
$$

$$
a - x = \sqrt{2}x + \sqrt{2}a
$$

$$
x(1 + \sqrt{2}) = a(1 - \sqrt{2})
$$

$$
x = a \frac{1 - \sqrt{2}}{1 + \sqrt{2}} = a(2\sqrt{2} - 3)
$$

### 3. Calculate the field for specific values

Given $a = 0.2 \ \mathrm{m}$, $y = 0.3 \ \mathrm{m}$, and $q = 2 \ \mu\mathrm{C}$. Assuming the point is on the y-axis ($x = 0$), we use the $\vec{E}(0, y)$ equations.

$$
k q = (9 \times 10^9) (2 \times 10^{-6}) = 18 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{V \cdot m/C}
$$

$$
(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2} = (0.2^2 + 0.3^2)^{3/2} = (0.13)^{3/2} \approx 0.04687 \ \mathrm{m^3}
$$

Calculating the components:

$$
\begin{align}
E_x &= -\frac{(18 \times 10^3)(0.2)}{0.04687} \approx -76.8 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{V/m} \\
E_y &= \frac{3(18 \times 10^3)(0.3)}{0.04687} \approx 345.6 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{V/m}
\end{align}
$$

### 4. Limit $y \gg a$

When evaluating the field on the y-axis for points very far from the charges, we apply the approximation $a^2 + y^2 \approx y^2$.

$$
\begin{align}
E_x(0, y) &\approx -\frac{k q a}{(y^2)^{3/2}} = -\frac{k q a}{y^3} \\
E_y(0, y) &\approx \frac{3 k q y}{(y^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{3 k q}{y^2}
\end{align}
$$

### 5. Point of zero field on the y-axis

To have a zero field point on the y-axis, both $E_x(0, y)$ and $E_y(0, y)$ must equal zero simultaneously.

We previously determined:

$$
E_x(0, y) = -\frac{k q a}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}}
$$

For $E_x$ to be zero, either $a = 0$ or $y \to \infty$. Since $a$ is a non-zero distance, $E_x \neq 0$ for any finite point on the y-axis.

## Final Result

1. General field formulas derived in step 1.
2. $\vec{E} = 0$ strictly at the point $(a(2\sqrt{2} - 3), 0)$.
3. $\vec{E}(0, 0.3) \approx (-76.8, 345.6) \ \mathrm{kV/m}$.
4. For $y \gg a$, $E_y \propto 1/y^2$ and $E_x \propto 1/y^3$.
5. No point of zero field exists on the y-axis (except at infinity).

## Interpretation

The condition for zero field only occurs between the charges because their individual fields must be directly opposed. The equilibrium point is pushed closer to the smaller charge ($+q$) because the $+2q$ charge exerts a stronger influence over distance. In the limit of $y \gg a$, the $E_x$ component decays much faster ($1/y^3$) than the $E_y$ component ($1/y^2$). Consequently, from far away along the y-axis, the system field is dominated by $E_y$ and behaves approximately like a single point charge of magnitude $3q$ located at the origin.
